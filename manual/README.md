# Morse Flipper Manual

This is the main help for Morse Flipper. The name is false advertising: no walruses are flipped, neither horizontally nor by vertical finger. The shorter on-device Help is the pocket version; its mirror is [internal-help.md](internal-help.md).

## First Steps

You will start with K and M. K is the longer one. Do not count dits and dahs; listen until the sound goes straight from your ears to your brain. Open `Training → Listening`.

For the first run, you will hear the two letters at about 20 WPM with gaps. Later, you can adjust this in `Settings → Listening`. The gaps are called `Farnsworth` timing. Characters stay brisk; the gaps do the mercy work. That trains the ear without making your hand pretend it is ready.

Answer with the joystick centre button, `OK` - just repeat what you hear. It beeps while held. You may send much slower than the prompt; keep the dit/dah ratio even and let speed come later. Answer slower if you must; just do not slow down what you listen to.

This input method is called _straight keying_: one button, one electrical contact, all timing by hand. Do 10-15 questions a day. Errors are shown in black. At roughly 90% correct, raise `Farnsworth` in `Settings → Listening`. Increasing Farnsworth narrows the gaps and gives you less time to negotiate with the sound. That is the point. When the spacing no longer props you up, move to the next lesson and lower it again if the new letter needs room.

Past the first few letters, you will hit plateaus. Each new letter will briefly make the old ones worse, because of course it will. This is normal. If you invest 10+ minutes a day, you might hear Morse in your dreams. That is normal: sleep is when your brain consolidates the pattern, sometimes with the volume left on. At this point you are learning.

## Learning The Alphabet

Keep character speed high. You want sound-to-letter reflex, not a little warehouse of dots and dashes in your head. Start at 30 WPM, or 25 WPM if 30 is too much, and stay there until lesson 10. After that, 20 WPM is acceptable if speed is getting in the way. Do not go lower just to flatter the score.

Use Farnsworth for breathing room. It changes gaps, not the letter sound. If K becomes "dash dot dash" in your head, you are counting. Make the exercise harder.

Do not let sending outrun listening. The straight key trainer and groups-of-five drills are useful, but only after you can hear the lesson properly.

## Using A Paddle

Use `iambic b` as the default paddle mode. It is Elekey-B, the common real-world default, and the least surprising place to start unless you already know better.

To try it on the Flipper, open `Settings → Keying`, leave `Input` as `buttons`, and set `Keyer` to `iambic b`. `OK` and `Back` become the paddles; use `Swap paddles` if dit and dah feel backwards. Settle this early. Changing paddle sides every other session is how you teach your hands rubbish. One awkward bit remains: when `Back` is a paddle, it cannot also be Back. The app shows this on the right: ⏴ Long-press `Left` to leave.

After the first few Koch listening lessons, the Flipper buttons may start feeling limiting. They are. I only added them so you can try the idea without extra hardware, not because rubber buttons make a decent key. Build a paddle when you can: two lever switches on a PCB wired to the Flipper GPIO are enough. Learn it early, and answer with a paddle early.
