# Resources

This is a small, opinionated map of things worth trying once Morse Flipper has got you past the first few letters. It is not a complete directory. If a resource teaches you to listen instead of count, gives you regular practice, or puts you near real operators without too much ceremony, it probably belongs here.

## Learning Tools

- [LCWO.net](https://lcwo.net/) is the common web baseline for Koch-style listening practice. Morse Flipper follows its lesson order so you can move between the Flipper and the browser without relearning the alphabet through a different lesson plan.
- [Vail](https://vailmorse.com/) is an open-source, browser-based internet Morse repeater. Morse Flipper can present itself as a Vail MIDI adapter, which is why the app bothers to implement the MIDI path fully rather than doing the usual keyboard-emulation nonsense. Vail is one of the better ways to practise live with other humans without making RF, antennas, propagation, and licensing part of the same evening.
- [VBand](https://hamradio.solutions/vband/) is another established internet CW practice service. It feels older and more self-contained than Vail, but it has users, a decoder, and enough shared expectations that it remains useful for checking whether your sending survives outside your own app. For VBand, set Morse Flipper to `Settings → USB → Connection: Keyboard` and `Paddle keys: [ ]`.
- [MorseKOB](https://sites.google.com/site/morsekob/morsekob25) and [Morse over IP](https://morse-code-over-ip.github.io/moip/) are closer to landline telegraphy than ham-band CW. They are worth a look if sounders, historic wire practice, and internet telegraph circuits appeal to you.
- [iCW over Mumble](https://lidscw.org/resources/practicing-cw-online-with-icw) is another internet-CW route. It sends Morse as audio through low-latency voice software. It is more fiddly than Vail or VBand, but some QRQ operators like the full-break-in feel.
- [University of Twente WebSDR](http://websdr.ewi.utwente.nl:8901/?tune=14044cw) is a real HF receiver on the internet. That direct link opens around 14.044 MHz in CW mode. Use the frequency box or the waterfall to tune a few kHz either way, keep the mode on CW/USB as appropriate, and listen for real band behaviour: fading, pileups, uneven fists, and ordinary HF mess.
- [Morse Walker](https://morsewalker.com/) is a browser pileup trainer with modes for ordinary pileups, POTA, CWT, and SST. Good once you know enough callsigns to be dangerous.
- [RufzXP](https://www.rufzxp.net/) is the classic callsign-copy trainer. It is not pretty. It does not need to be. Callsigns are where many learners discover that knowing letters is not the same as copying radio.
- [Morse Runner](https://github.com/w7sst/MorseRunner) is the contest simulator everyone keeps recommending because it still does the job. Use it after you have some copy speed, otherwise it is just a stress generator with a score box. [Download here](https://github.com/w7sst/MorseRunner/releases/download/v1.85.4/Morse.Runner.1.85.4.zip).

## Games

- [Morsle](https://morsle.fun/) is Wordle by ear. The daily word is played in Morse, fast at first, then less rude as you miss.
- [Morse Codle](https://morse-codle.com/) is another Wordle-like Morse game, this time focused on tapping or holding the pattern for letters.
- [Morse Invaders](https://morseinvaders.com/) is the obvious arcade idea and therefore correct: identify or send targets before they become your problem.
- [Morse Walker](https://morsewalker.com/) is listed above as a learning tool, but it is also a game in the useful sense: short runs, obvious failure, and enough pressure to teach something.
- [Morse Runner](https://github.com/w7sst/MorseRunner) is contesting as a small Windows simulator. Start gently. Turning on every impairment at once is a character flaw, not training. [Download here](https://github.com/w7sst/MorseRunner/releases/download/v1.85.4/Morse.Runner.1.85.4.zip).
- [Morse Mania](https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=net.countrymania.morse) is a mobile app with receiving and sending levels, including letters, numbers, prosigns, Q-codes, abbreviations, words, callsigns, and phrases.
- [MORSE](https://alexvscoding.itch.io/morse) is a modern strategy game built around telegraphy and different key types. It is good outreach material, which is a polite way of saying it looks less like homework.
- [Morse C.O.D.](https://seadads.itch.io/morse-cod) is a small arcade-style game where targets are handled by Morse input.
- [Morse Mania Web](https://thomdotcom.itch.io/morse-mania) is the browser version of the straight-key practice idea, useful when installing yet another app feels excessive.

## Courses

Different people need different amounts of structure. Some learn well alone with short daily drills and a tracker. Others need a fixed class, a human advisor, and the mild shame of being expected somewhere twice a week. Both are normal. Pick the failure mode you can actually survive.

- [CW Academy](https://cwops.org/cw-academy/) - free. Run by CWops, with no membership requirement. The programme is structured, advisor-led, and offered in sessions through the year. The [course options](https://cwops.org/cw-academy/cw-academy-options/) expect the practical kit: good internet, a computer, camera/mic, a paddle, a keyer or radio with sidetone, and daily practice. Straight keys, bugs, and cooties are explicitly not used for those courses. This is not unreasonable; they are trying to teach timing and conversational CW, not adjudicate every mechanical contraption in the drawer.
- [Morse Code Ninja](https://morsecode.ninja/learn/) - free. Mostly self-teaching: YouTube, downloads, podcasts, practice sets, and a great deal of material for different stages. It is especially good for non-interactive practice: dog walking, driving, walking, or being wedged into public transport with the rest of civilisation. The usual pattern is simple: it plays Morse at you, waits, then tells you what you just heard. You judge yourself, which is both efficient and slightly dangerous. It suits people who can build their own routine and actually follow it. If you need a human to keep you honest, pair it with a class or a regular sked.
- [Long Island CW Club](https://longislandcwclub.org/) - paid membership/donation. LICW is instructor-led over Zoom, with many weekly classes and a path towards real QSOs. It is a good fit if you want structure, repetition, and other people around while the skill is still fragile.
- [LCWO.net](https://lcwo.net/) - free. It is not a class, but it is the reference self-paced web course for Koch-style learning, word training, callsign training, and tracking your progress. If you use Morse Flipper for the bus and LCWO at the desk, you are not doing two unrelated things.

## Other

- [SKCC](https://skccgroup.com/) is the Straight Key Century Club: a large mechanical-key CW group with awards, events, and a strong bias towards straight keys, bugs, and sideswipers. Go there when you want manual keying to be a community rather than just a thing you are struggling with alone.
- [FISTS](https://www.fists.org/) is the International Morse Preservation Society. The useful bit for learners is not only the name; local chapters and resources tend to be friendly to ordinary-speed CW.
- [Reverse Beacon Network](https://www.reversebeacon.net/) is a network of receivers that report which CW and digital stations they hear, where, and how strongly. Once you are transmitting on the air, it can tell you whether your signal is getting out.
- [Morse Code Ninja Resources](https://www.morsecode.ninja/resources/index.html) is a broader link collection covering software, online practice, books, advice, hardware, and games. Use it when this page runs out.
- [CWops Resources](https://cwops.org/cw-resources/) collects practice material, software links, and class references. It is a practical place to look after CW Academy has got your attention.

## Contests

- [CWT](https://cwops.org/cwops-tests/) is the CWops weekly sprint and the first contest-like thing worth understanding. It is fast, short, regular, and full of people who know what they are doing. You can search-and-pounce before you run. Reported scores go to [3830scores.com](https://www.3830scores.com/); CWops asks for CWT results by time segment and within 48 hours.
- [SST](https://www.k1usn.com/sst.html) and [MST](https://internationalcwcouncil.org/mst-contest/) exist because there is a gap between "I can copy Morse" and "I enjoy being hit by 38 WPM contest traffic". SST is the slow-speed version, capped around 20 WPM and meant for newer operators. MST sits around 20-25 WPM. Both use short exchanges and public score posting on [3830scores.com](https://www.3830scores.com/); the point is not pretending to be elite, it is learning to call CQ, exchange cleanly, and survive small amounts of pressure.
- [CQ WW](https://cqww.com/) is the large worldwide DX contest. The CW weekend is the last full weekend of November. The goal is contacts across DXCC entities and CQ zones, so the bands can sound like every antenna on earth has decided to speak at once.
- [CQ WPX](https://cqwpx.com/rules/) is the prefix contest. In CW, it usually lands at the end of May. Prefixes are the multiplier, which means callsigns become even more important than usual, and usual was already quite a lot.
- [IARU HF World Championship](https://www.arrl.org/iaru-hf-world-championship) is a 24-hour contest on the second full weekend of July. Ordinary stations send report plus ITU zone; headquarters stations send report plus society abbreviation. It is compact, international, and much less of a weekend-devouring beast than the 48-hour monsters.
- [WRTC 2026](https://www.wrtc2026.org/) happens in Great Britain, in the UK, from 8-13 July 2026, alongside the IARU HF Championship operating period. WRTC runs on a four-year cycle and is the radio-contesting Olympics in the only sensible meaning of that phrase: qualified two-person teams, similar stations, referees, and everyone trying to remove excuses from the result. The public can still work the WRTC stations during the IARU event, which is a rare chance to hear very serious operators in a controlled circus.
