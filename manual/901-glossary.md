# Glossary

This is not a complete ham-radio dictionary. It is the vocabulary Morse Flipper uses often enough to justify a local translation layer, with just enough context to stop the usual small misunderstandings from breeding.

## 5NN

5NN is CW shorthand for 599, a very good RST signal report. In contests it is sent so often that it has become partly report, partly ritual, and partly proof that radio operators will optimise even their exaggerations.

See also: [RST](#rst), [Contesting](#contesting)

## 73

73 means best regards, usually at the end of a contact.

See also: [QSO](#qso)

## Abbreviations

CW abbreviations are the small operating words that keep a contact copyable by ear. They are not decoration; they are compression for a medium where every extra character costs time and attention. The built-in Flipper help has the longer list under [A complete Morse contact](internal-help.md#a-complete-morse-contact), and the same help is available inside the app from the Help menu.

Start by recognising FER, DE, R, K, ES, GA, GM, GE, FB, and 73. That is enough to stop a simple QSO from looking like someone dropped a Scrabble bag into the radio.

See also: [A complete Morse contact](internal-help.md#a-complete-morse-contact), [QSO](#qso), [CW](#cw), [Prosign](#prosign)

## BK

BK means break. In a normal CW contact it is often a compact "back to you", especially when both stations already know what is happening and nobody needs a full ceremonial over. It is also a prosign when sent as one joined sign.

See also: [Prosign](#prosign), [QSO](#qso)

## Bug

A bug is a semi-automatic mechanical key. It usually makes repeated dits mechanically while dahs are still sent by hand. A good bug fist has a pleasing swing; a bad one sounds like a clock escaping downstairs.

See also: [Key](#key), [Fist](#fist), [Swing](#swing)

## Callsign

A callsign identifies a radio station. Amateur callsigns are issued by national authorities and used during contacts, contests, logs, and QSLs. Think of it as a government-issued username, but with more aluminium tubing involved.

See also: [QSO](#qso), [CQ](#cq), [DE](#de), [QSL](#qsl), [Contesting](#contesting)

## Character Speed

Character speed is the speed inside each Morse character: dits, dahs, and the tiny gaps between them. Farnsworth spacing can make the overall text slower without making each character itself slow and mushy. That distinction matters; slow characters invite counting, and counting is how the trap gets built.

See also: [WPM](#wpm), [Farnsworth](#farnsworth), [Counting](#counting), [Koch Method](#koch-method), [LCWO](#lcwo)

## Contesting

Contesting is radio competition. Operators try to make as many valid contacts as possible under a contest's rules, usually with short exchanges, fast sending, and very little patience for decorative prose. It is not always friendly to beginners, but it is excellent for learning what compact CW sounds like when nobody is wasting syllables.

See also: [5NN](#5nn), [RST](#rst), [QSO](#qso)

## Copy

To copy Morse is to receive it and write, type, or remember what was sent. Good copy means the brain heard the characters and kept up; it does not mean a software decoder had a lucky afternoon.

See also: [Counting](#counting), [LCWO](#lcwo), [Koch Method](#koch-method), [Farnsworth](#farnsworth)

## Counting

Counting is the beginner habit of translating sound into dots and dashes before deciding on the letter: dah dit dah, therefore K. It feels clever and is exactly the wrong bargain. Keep character speed high, use Farnsworth for breathing room, and make the sound land as the letter, like a multiplication table answer. If you catch yourself counting, the exercise is too comfortable.

See also: [Character Speed](#character-speed), [Farnsworth](#farnsworth), [Koch Method](#koch-method), [LCWO](#lcwo)

## CQ

CQ is a general call: roughly, "anyone listening?" A station calling CQ is inviting a contact. In ordinary use you will often hear something like CQ CQ DE YO3GND, meaning "anyone, from YO3GND".

See also: [DE](#de), [QSO](#qso), [Callsign](#callsign)

## CW

CW means continuous wave, but in ham radio it normally means Morse Code operation. The radio signal is often an on/off keyed carrier; the culture around it includes listening, sending, timing, abbreviations, contests, and a surprising amount of strongly held opinion about small noises.

See also: [Dit](#dit), [Dah](#dah), [Element](#element), [TX and RX](#tx-and-rx), [Sidetone](#sidetone)

## Dah

A dah is the long Morse element. In standard timing it lasts three dits. It is a sound first, not a dash to be counted off a chart.

See also: [Dit](#dit), [Element](#element), [WPM](#wpm), [Counting](#counting)

## DE

DE means from. CQ DE YO3GND means "calling anyone, from YO3GND". It is one of the little operating words that makes a CW contact readable without full sentences getting in the way.

See also: [CQ](#cq), [Callsign](#callsign), [QSO](#qso)

## Dit

A dit is the short Morse element. It is one timing unit long. If you find yourself counting dits and dahs in your head, the exercise is probably too comfortable.

See also: [Dah](#dah), [Element](#element), [WPM](#wpm), [Counting](#counting)

## Dual-Lever Paddle

A dual-lever paddle has separate dit and dah levers, and both can be pressed at the same time. That squeeze is what makes iambic keying possible. The paddle is just contacts; the keyer is the thing making tidy timing out of your finger decisions.

See also: [Paddle](#paddle), [Keyer](#keyer), [Iambic A](#iambic-a), [Iambic B](#iambic-b)

## Element

An element is one dit or one dah. Gaps are the silences between elements, characters, and words. Morse is not just marks; the spaces are part of the message, which is rude but true.

See also: [Dit](#dit), [Dah](#dah), [WPM](#wpm), [Farnsworth](#farnsworth)

## Farnsworth

Farnsworth spacing keeps characters fast but stretches the gaps between them. It gives the learner time to answer without teaching slow, countable Morse. Use it as mercy, not as a hammock.

See also: [Character Speed](#character-speed), [WPM](#wpm), [Counting](#counting), [Koch Method](#koch-method), [LCWO](#lcwo)

## Fist

A fist is an operator's personal sending style. A clean fist can be recognisable and still be correct.

See also: [Straight Key](#straight-key), [Bug](#bug), [Swing](#swing)

## Ham

A ham is a licensed amateur radio operator. The word also carries the culture: callsigns, contacts, bands, radios, antennas, logs, contests, QSL cards, and occasionally making a simple thing sound more technical than necessary.

Hams usually have a quiet duty to proselytise the hobby. Once you are licensed, the reasons will be explained at the first entirely respectable cult meeting, along with why one antenna is never enough.

See also: [Callsign](#callsign), [QSO](#qso), [QSL](#qsl)

## Iambic A

Iambic A is an electronic keyer mode for dual-lever paddles. During a squeeze it alternates dits and dahs, but after release it is conservative: it normally stops after the current element.

See also: [Iambic B](#iambic-b), [Dual-Lever Paddle](#dual-lever-paddle), [Keyer](#keyer)

## Iambic B

Iambic B is the more eager iambic mode. During squeeze and release timing it can add one extra opposite element after the current element finishes. That is not a glitch; it is the keyer doing exactly what the timing asked for, which is not always what your hand thought it asked for.

See also: [Iambic A](#iambic-a), [Dual-Lever Paddle](#dual-lever-paddle), [Keyer](#keyer), [Paddle](#paddle)

## Key

A key is the thing used to send Morse by closing a contact. A straight key is manual. A paddle feeds a keyer. A bug sits somewhere in the mechanical middle and looks pleased with itself.

See also: [Straight Key](#straight-key), [Paddle](#paddle), [Bug](#bug), [Keyer](#keyer)

## Keyer

A keyer is the timing machine between paddle contacts and Morse output. It reads dit and dah inputs, applies WPM and mode rules, and emits clean key-down/key-up timing. Treat it like a small musical instrument, because that is how it behaves.

See also: [Paddle](#paddle), [Iambic A](#iambic-a), [Iambic B](#iambic-b), [Ultimatic](#ultimatic), [WPM](#wpm)

## Koch Method

The Koch method teaches Morse by starting with a tiny character set at real character speed, then adding more characters as the learner becomes reliable. The method is about recognition by sound, not memorising a dot-dash inventory. Strictly, Koch is the method, not one holy character order. When people say "the Koch order", they often mean this older suggested sequence:

```text
K M R S U A P T L O W I . N J E F 0 Y , V G 5 / Q 9 Z H 3 8 B ? 4 2 7 C 1 D 6 X <BT> <SK> <AR>
```

Morse Flipper does not use that sequence. It uses the LCWO order. Do not invent your own unless you want to be incompatible with all the Morse [games and tools](910-resources.md) available to make your training days more tolerable.

See also: [LCWO](#lcwo), [Counting](#counting), [Farnsworth](#farnsworth), [Character Speed](#character-speed)

## LCWO

[LCWO.net](https://lcwo.net/) means Learn CW Online, a long-standing web trainer for learning Morse by sound. Its lesson order is different from the older Koch sequence:

```text
K M U R E S N A P T L W I . J Z = F O Y , V G 5 / Q 9 2 H 3 8 B ? 4 7 C 1 D 6 0 X
```

It is Koch-shaped rather than canonical Koch: real character speed, small starting pool, one new character at a time, with a practical lesson sequence that many learners and tools recognise. Morse Flipper follows this LCWO order so you can move between the Flipper and LCWO without discovering, too late, that your personal alphabet is a private little island.

See also: [Koch Method](#koch-method), [Character Speed](#character-speed), [Farnsworth](#farnsworth), [Counting](#counting), [Copy](#copy)

## Paddle

A paddle is a side-operated key with separate dit and dah contacts. Unlike a straight key, it needs a keyer to generate the timing. The paddle tells the keyer what you want; the keyer decides the exact marks and gaps.

See also: [Keyer](#keyer), [Dual-Lever Paddle](#dual-lever-paddle), [Single-Lever Paddle](#single-lever-paddle), [Iambic A](#iambic-a), [Iambic B](#iambic-b), [Ultimatic](#ultimatic)

## Prosign

A prosign is a procedural Morse sign sent as one joined symbol, without normal letter gaps between its parts. They are operating punctuation, not ordinary words. SOS is the best-known example. SK ends a transmission. BK means break, often "your turn". The important part is that a prosign is sent as one unit; the gap is not where the printed letters suggest it should be.

See also: [BK](#bk), [QSO](#qso), [CW](#cw)

## QRP

QRP means low-power operation. CW and QRP fit well together because Morse is narrow, efficient, and annoyingly capable when the antenna and operator are not terrible. Low power is not magic; it is just where good operating and good antennas stop being optional.

See also: [CW](#cw), [QSO](#qso)

## QRQ

QRQ asks for faster sending. It also gets used as shorthand for high-speed CW. Use it carefully; the correct answer may be a faster operator, not a braver menu setting.

See also: [QRS](#qrs), [WPM](#wpm), [Copy](#copy)

## QRS

QRS asks the other station to send more slowly. It is a useful thing to know before pride starts writing cheques your ears cannot cash.

See also: [QRQ](#qrq), [WPM](#wpm), [Copy](#copy)

## QSL

QSL means confirmed or acknowledged. A QSL card is a confirmation of a contact, part record, and part souvenir. Some operators still care deeply about them, and honestly, having a card for a radio contact is a charmingly physical sort of nonsense.

See also: [QSO](#qso), [Callsign](#callsign)

## QSO

A QSO is a radio contact between stations. In a friendly chat it can be a conversation; in a contest it may be callsigns, reports, and a serial number, then straight on to the next one. CW QSOs are usually full of abbreviations because the other operator is copying by ear, not reading your novel.

See also: [Abbreviations](#abbreviations), [CQ](#cq), [DE](#de), [BK](#bk), [RST](#rst), [Callsign](#callsign), [73](#73)

## RST

RST is a signal report: Readability, Strength, Tone. CW reports often collapse into 599, and then into 5NN, because saving two characters apparently matters when everyone is already sending at warp nonsense.

See also: [5NN](#5nn), [QSO](#qso), [Contesting](#contesting)

## Sidetone

Sidetone is the local audio you hear while keying. It is feedback for your hand, not proof that RF went out or USB reached the computer. If sidetone is late, your hand learns the wrong timing, which is an impressively petty way for software to sabotage you.

See also: [Keyer](#keyer), [WPM](#wpm), [CW](#cw)

## Single-Lever Paddle

A single-lever paddle moves left or right around one lever. It can select dit or dah, but it cannot truly squeeze both at once. Many high-speed operators like single-lever paddles because there is less squeeze timing to get subtly wrong.

See also: [Paddle](#paddle), [Dual-Lever Paddle](#dual-lever-paddle), [Keyer](#keyer)

## Straight Key

A straight key is the classic up-and-down telegraph key. The operator controls all timing by hand: marks, gaps, character spacing, word spacing, the lot. It is honest, unforgiving, and very good at showing you exactly which part of your rhythm is lying.

See also: [Key](#key), [Fist](#fist), [Swing](#swing)

## Swing

Swing is rhythmic unevenness in sending, often heard with bugs or very personal straight-key fists. A little can be character. Too much becomes work for the poor soul copying it.

See also: [Fist](#fist), [Bug](#bug), [Straight Key](#straight-key)

## TX and RX

TX means transmit. RX means receive. Morse Flipper uses both in the radio screens, and blocks TX where the chosen frequency or firmware rules do not allow it. These are not moral categories; they are directions.

See also: [VFO](#vfo), [CW](#cw), [QRP](#qrp)

## Ultimatic

Ultimatic is a non-iambic squeeze keyer mode. If both paddles are held, the last paddle pressed wins. It is not merely "iambic but odd"; it is its own rule set, and some hands genuinely prefer it.

See also: [Iambic A](#iambic-a), [Iambic B](#iambic-b), [Dual-Lever Paddle](#dual-lever-paddle), [Keyer](#keyer)

## VFO

VFO means variable frequency oscillator. In practice here it is the selected radio frequency. If the CC1101 cannot lock there, the app will refuse to pretend otherwise.

See also: [TX and RX](#tx-and-rx)

## WPM

WPM means words per minute. Morse timing normally uses the standard word PARIS as the measuring stick because it represents a conventional word length in Morse timing. At 20 WPM, a dit is 60 ms, a dah is 180 ms, and the tiny gaps matter more than beginners expect.

See also: [Character Speed](#character-speed), [Farnsworth](#farnsworth), [Element](#element), [Counting](#counting)
